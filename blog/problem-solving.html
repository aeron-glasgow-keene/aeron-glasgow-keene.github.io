An Exploration of Problem Solving Techniques

A time when I was blocked on a simple problem...

I struggled quite a bit with the "Gradebook" and "Built-in Methods" Kata exercises. I was using pseudocode a lot to explain to myself the logical steps to solve the problems, but my code kept not working despite being logically sound (at least in my head). The issue was that I didn't know what I didn't know about syntax and JavaScript methods, so I couldn't easily break down what the problem was. Console.log() sometimes helped here to tell me when I had done correctly halfway through my code, but I couldn't always get that to work. I felt frustrated during the process and ultimately resorted to ChatGPT to answer the problem and break down the steps so I could understand where I went wrong. If I learned anything from this process it's that ChatGPT is quite reliable but isn't nearly as satisfying as solving a problem without it.

A time when I elegantly solved a problem...

Somehow I did everything in the JavaScript Olympics in just over half an hour, mostly getting everything right first time without needing to Google anything. I found JS much easier after doing the rest of the Kata exercises; all I needed to do was jump in and "try something", using the error messages in the npm test to adjust, and my previous answers to the other exercises to remind me of syntax/methods.

Reflecting on how confident I feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes:

  • Pseudocode
    • Reasonably confident. I found it a little tricky to get my head around at first - mainly because I am not yet very familiar with what JavaScript can and can't do. But with practice it is becoming one of the best ways to mitigate my tendency to multi-task and instead focus on one thing at a time.
  • Trying something:
    • Very. Jumping in and working by trial-and-error is my go-to methodology.
  • Rubber ducky method:
    • So far I have been getting by without strictly using this method, but I often think of the concept generally when writing pseudocode to break down a confusing problem to its understandable components.
  • Reading error messages:
    • It depends on the error message. Usually I can find the relevant piece of information within error messages in Terminal etc., but sometimes it needs a Google for me to understand it.
  • Console.logging:
    • I understand the usefulness of this and am reasonably confident with applying it, but I haven't been using it very much (it stopped working for me in terminal halfway through the Kata exercises, still not sure why)
  • Googling:
    • 10000%
  • Asking your peers for help:
    • Stranger danger! I do feel a reluctance to asking for help from my peers generally. Although being stuck and frustrated on a problem for more than 1x Pomodoro is usually enough to offset any embarrassment I might feel.
  • Asking coaches for help:
    • Similar story as above. Sometimes it takes a lot for me to admit I need help, but when it comes to it I'm generally good at reminding myself it's totally fine and normal to not be a superhuman coding machine by week 4 of a web development course. I feel slightly less resistance to asking a coach (who I'm paying to be there when I need help) than asking my peers.
  • Improving your process with reflection:
    • Reflection is something that I think helps with retention of new ideas and getting familiar with new processes, but I haven't noticeably found it directly useful for problem solving.

Reflecting on a time I was reluctant to ask for help.

As mentioned above, I sometimes find it embarrassing to admit that I need help. One example of this is in the recent JavaScript Kata exercises: I would frequently come up with the correct logical solution but the code wouldn't work due to incorrect syntax. I would use all my other problem solving methods before asking for help.

This reluctance is probably due to my social conditioning of needing to "fit in" and/or be seen as smart/cool/etc. by my peers. No one else was asking for help on these exercises in Discord and I didn't want to be the first, even though if I think about it, it might have been helpful for other people to see.

One thing I could try next time I am struggling on something is to remind myself of the opportunity this could provide other people to learn-by-teaching, and see if anyone in my team can see the issue with my code.
