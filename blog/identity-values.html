Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

I recently found a mouse up at the land where my Mum and stepdad live. They live in the foothills of the Tararuas, where rodents are a real threat to native flora and fauna. It didn't scamper away as I approached, and I was able to scoop it up into my hands. I then realised I was in a situation conflicting to me as an animal lover. I brought it to my stepdad, as I was unsure what the best thing would be to do with it - or rather, I knew but didn't want to do it myself. I guess I was hoping he'd agree with my idea to put it in a box and release it down in the suburbs where they theoretically don't pose as much of a threat to nature. Once he pointed out that it was likely poisoned (hence not running at my approach), the best thing to do became clear. I couldn't reconcile the conflict in my values as quickly as he could, so I gave it to him and he drowned it in a watering can. I remember looking into its eyes before handing it over... I draw some solace in the fact we were likely ending its suffering, and that I showed it some measure of compassion, before doing what was best for the greater good. Reflecting on it now, I can see that acting compassionately and acting logically are both core values of mine, though the latter (usually) comes out on top in a conflict.

My identity and values are absolutely a product of the people around me during my life. I am fortunate to have been raised by parents who were both kind-hearted, artistic and relatively introverted people, and there's no doubting that I assimilated a lot of they way they thought about themselves and the world at large.

Looking back, I can also see that my peers (and/or, the need to fit in) also did a lot of the work of molding my identity. I wasn't actively into the arts, despite having the propensity for it, until my high school friends effectively peer-pressured me into pretending that I loved rock and emo music and had the same taste as themâ€¦ But in the process, I started to legitimately enjoy it, and ended up becoming more obsessed with becoming a musician than everyone else around me. For most of the rest of my life since then I believed being a musician was the only viable career path for me (reality has since insisted otherwise, resulting in the ongoing identity crisis I am currently in).

Another crucial element to the forming of my values/identity has been stumbling into the world of men's groups. Describing my journey with this would involve divulging more personal details than I am willing to share on the internet, but my involvement with and support from communities dedicated to fostering healthy, human connection, healing and growth has been fundamental to keeping my sanity as a highly empathetic person in the insane world we live in.

I have also recently explored the Burning Man community culture and values. I'm still working out what I do and don't like about it, but I definitely appreciate the idea of mindful/responsible hedonism, which is a key part of my journey in what you might call identity reforming. As are my recent travel experiences in Europe, further solidifying my want/need for adventure and novelty in my life right now.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

My bandmate and I clashed ideologically and creativity quite a bit during my time as a performing artist. Our personalities clashed and we pushed each other's buttons at band practice. For the most part, my strategy was to either find ways to productively communicate my issues, or to hold in the frustration until I knew how to communicate it cleanly. Sometimes that worked, but mostly what ended up happening was that I would hold it in until I burst out in an unhelpful comment/expression of frustration, which would force us into talking about it. Fortunately we were both good at talking about it once feelings were expressed and we had calmed down. In a sense this was effective, as it eventually got both of us on the same page that we needed to be. In another sense, it caused needless tension and outbursts of emotion that was unconducive to a creative/performance environment, and negatively impacted those around us.

These days I am much better at recognising what conflicts are going on within myself earlier on, and recognising the importance of communicating this clearly early on. I do recognise the need to develop my "Non-Violent Communication" skills further, and I'm looking forward to the opportunities to do that during this course.
